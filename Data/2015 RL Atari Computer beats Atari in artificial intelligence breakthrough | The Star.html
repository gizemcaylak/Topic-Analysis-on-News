        Computer beats Atari in artificial intelligence breakthrough

        By Kate AllenScience and Technology reporter
        Wed., Feb. 25, 2015timer4 min. read

        Thirteen months ago Google paid somewhere in the neighbourhood of half a billion dollars to acquire a company called DeepMind, a U.K.-based startup with fewer than 100 employees.

        A study published Wednesday in the journal Nature shows why: researchers at the company have designed an artificial agent — a computer algorithm, essentially — that learned to play 49 different Atari arcade games and performed at or above the level of humans in more than half of them.

        The agent, called deep Q-network or DQN, operates very differently from artificial intelligence systems like Watson, the IBM computer that won on Jeopardy in 2011. Watson was loaded with four terabytes worth of data. In contrast, the DQN algorithm beat the Atari games had no preprogrammed information of any kind — it truly learned how to play.

        “All it gets access to is raw pixel inputs and the game score. From there, it has to figure out what it controls in the game world, how to get points and how to master the game, just like playing the game directly,” DeepMind founder Demis Hassabis told reporters.

        DQN mastered games like Breakout and Space Invaders with no intervention from the scientists in between the different challenges.

        “What they’ve been doing is groundbreaking,” said Brendan Frey, a machine learning scientist at the University of Toronto.

        The breakthrough, Frey said, was combining two modes of machine learning. One of them, called “deep learning,” was brought to prominence in large part by Geoffrey Hinton, a University of Toronto researcher who also now works part-time for Google.

        The search giant and its competitors have raced to snatch up artificial intelligence gurus in recent years with high hopes that the technology has the potential to transform everything from virtual assistants like iPhone’s Siri to genome research.

        “Ultimately, the idea is that if this algorithm can race a car in a racing game, then also potentially, with a few extra tweaks, it should be able to drive a real car,” said DeepMind’s Hassabis, though he stressed that such advances are years away. “That’s the ultimate aim of these general learning algorithms. In theory, you should be able to just give it a new data stream and it should be able to adapt to it.”

        Hassabis is a former neuroscientist, and DQN uses an architecture known as neural networks, a “deep learning” system inspired by the structure of a human brain. Artificial neural networks process information through several layers of nodes, extracting more and more context at each layer.

        Hinton is considered by many to be the godfather of deep neural networks. He was at the forefront of a tiny group that continued studying the technique after most scientists gave up on it — a decision that was vindicated when they finally proved it could work.

        “This is an amazing result,” Hinton told the Star from California, where he has worked part-time since Google acquired his own startup in 2013.

        That same year Facebook hired Yann LeCun, another pioneer in the field, to found an AI lab. Deep learning has already proven highly successful at tasks like photo recognition, a function with obvious benefits to search engines and social networks.

        But those successes relied on combining neural networks with a technique called “supervised learning,” whereby the researcher tells the algorithm whether it got an answer wrong or right.

        DeepMind’s breakthrough was to combine neural networks with another technique, “reinforcement learning,” that had languished in the lab but that more closely mimics human learning: by drawing on rewards or penalties from past experiences, the algorithm alters how it behaves in the future.

        In the case of DQN, that means processing the game screens it just saw (the experience) and the score (the reward) and modifying its actions without any input from its human minders. It may sound simple but applying reinforcement learning to sophisticated tasks has bedevilled AI researchers.

        “There’s a delay between your actions and the feedback that you’re getting, and the feedback doesn’t tell you what the action should have been ... it’s much, much harder,” said Yoshua Bengio, another Canadian neural net pioneer.

        Bengio, head of the Montreal Institute for Learning Algorithms, described DeepMind’s work as incremental but important.

        In the video game showdown, DQN was not perfect: it performed worse than a human in 20 of the 49 games, and poorest on games like Ms. Pac-Man, which require more advanced planning. The next stage will be to set DQN to work on 3-D games. But eventually the algorithm, and reinforcement learning in general, could do much more, from commanding smart cars to reading the human genome.

        “The reason everybody is excited about this is not because it can play video games,” said Andrew Delong, another U of T machine learning researcher. “It’s going to be big in robotics. It’s going to be big in finance. It’s going to be big in the military, for better or for worse.”
